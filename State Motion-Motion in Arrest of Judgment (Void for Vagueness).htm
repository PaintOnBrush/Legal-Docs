 DEFENDANT'S MOTION IN ARREST OF JUDGMENT

COMES NOW the defendant, pursuant to O.C.G.A. § 17-9-61, subsequent to judgment, without waiving any other rights to which he is entitled, and respectfully moves this Court to arrest the judgment entered against him on May 21, 1992 in the above-styled case on the following ground:

The judgment which was returned against defendant is predicated solely upon the defendant's alleged violation of Section 16-10-1 of the Official Code of Georgia in that, as a public officer, he violated his oath of office. Said statute is unconstitutionally vague, and said judgment thus amounts to a denial of defendant's right to due process under the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution.

1.

The defendant was indicted on the charges of, one, Theft by Receiving Stolen Property and, two, Violation of Oath of by a Public Officer (O.C.G.A. § 16-10-1). At trial, this Court directed a verdict of acquittal with respect to the first charge. A duly sworn and empaneled jury returned a verdict of "guilty" against the defendant with respect to the second charge.

 

2.

The Due Process clause of the United States Constitution as applied to the State of Georgia via the Fourteenth Amendment mandates that "[f]or any particular act or conduct to constitute a criminal offense the statute defining the offense, or some other law of the State must in express terms declare such conduct to be a violation of the law or provide that it be punished as a criminal offense." Wood v. State, 219 Ga. 509, 511 (1963).

3.

Furthermore, "a law is void on its face if it is so vague that persons of `common intelligence must necessarily guess at its meaning and differ as to its application.'" Gouge v. City of Snellville, 249 Ga. 91, 9_ (1982) (quoting Connally v. General Construction Co., 269 U.S. 385 (1926)).

4.

There are two evils that the "void-for-vagueness" doctrine seeks to avoid. The first is the "arbitrary and discriminatory enforcement" of penal statutes, e.g., Kolender v. Lawson, 461 U.S. 352, 357 (1983). The second is that "[n]o one may be required at peril of life, liberty or property to speculate as to the meaning of penal statutes. All are entitled to be informed as to what the State commands or forbids." Lanzetta v. New Jersey, 306 U.S. 451 (1939).

5.

O.C.G.A. § 16-10-1 and the Oath of office of a police officer of Lithonia Georgia, which is by implication incorporated into this code section, are void on the ground that they are unconstitutionally vague. The language in question is so vague that persons of common intelligence have to guess as to its meaning. U.S. CONST. amend. XIV, § 1.

6.

Moreover, in this case, both aforementioned constitutional evils have manifested themselves.

A. First, the jury's interpretation of O.C.G.A. § 16-10-1 and the defendant's oath of office was the product of unmitigated speculation.

(1) The statute in question holds that "[a]ny public officer who willfully and intentionally violates the terms of his oath as prescribed by law shall, upon conviction thereof, be punished by imprisonment for not less than one nor more than five years." Defendant was indicted for violating this provision by "appropriating to his own use a handgun received in the course of his duty as a police officer."

(2) The defendant's oath of office states:

I do solemnly swear that I will well and truly demean myself as a police officer of the city of Lithonia and that I will faithfully enforce the charter and ordinances of said city of Lithonia to the best of my ability, without fear or favor, and will in all my actions as police officer act as I believe for the best interests of said city, so help me God.

When the jury charges are considered together with the indictment, it is clear that a jury of laymen was given the authority to interpret the cryptic language of the defendant's oath of office without legal guidance, and with no clear cut definitions of the terms of the defendant's oath of office.

(3) Upon considering each element of the oath, the act of appropriating contraband to one's personal use is not expressly incorporated into the language.

(a) The act of "demeaning" oneself does not create a legal duty upon the affiant because a specific act or omission is not defined. The term "demean" is a general term, meaning to humble oneself or lower oneself in status. WEBSTER'S NEW WORLD DICTIONARY OF THE AMERICAN LANGUAGE (1960). This general term is obviously precatory language which can in no way imply a legal duty.

(b) The duty to enforce the charter and ordinances of the city of Lithonia is also a general term of precatory nature. It is, moreover, inapposite to the facts of this case. Defendant was not charged (nor was such ever alleged at trial) with a failure to enforce a municipal law. The "without fear or favor" language is obvious reference to the acts of extortion or bribery. No reasonable reading of this language could put any reasonable person on notice that it proscribes the personal use of contraband weapons.

(c) The admonition to act in the best interests of the city is yet another example of an admonition of a precatory nature.

B. Finally, O.C.G.A. § 16-10-1 was unquestionably applied to defendant in an arbitrary and discriminatory manner. Evidence was presented at trial to support the allegation that the actions of the defendant which resulted in his arrest and indictment--to wit: the pawning of a contraband weapon for a nominal amount of U.S. currency--was not isolated to the defendant. Indeed, the defendant would submit that if his actions do amount to felonious conduct, then he has been singled out for prosecution and the majority of peace officers in the state of Georgia have, at one time or another, engaged in much graver felonious activities. Therefore, the prosecution of defendant under this theory is an example of arbitrary and discriminatory prosecutorial conduct.

7.

Additionally, and if this honorable Court does find that a jury could interpret the language of the statute and oath to address the acts of defendant, then defendant would submit that such a broad scope of interpretation is of even graver concern. If such "dragnet" language can be used to proscribe any act which does not come within its express terms, then what public officer can feel secure in executing his duties of office? Indeed, what are the delimitations of felonious conduct in this context? Should any indiscrete act of a public officer--which need not even amount to a crime under the laws of this state or nation--henceforth be brought before a jury of lay people who must interpret for themselves the delphic and aspirational language of an oath of office to determine whether this act rises to the level of a felony? The defendant respectfully submits that by any standard of fairness and equity this should not be the case.

8.

The defendant concedes that statutory language cannot be expected to define conduct with "mathematical certainty," Grayned v. City of Rockford, 408 U.S. 104 (1972), and that a legislature cannot predict every possible permutation of a fact pattern arising with regard to a particular statute. See generally W.R. LAFAVE & A.W. SCOTT, JR., CRIMINAL LAW (2d ed. 1986). Nevertheless, the due process clause mandates that, at minimum, a person be placed on notice that conduct which, for non-public officials does not amount to a crime under the laws of Georgia or of the United States, be delineated in such a manner that reasonable men and women would know with reasonable certainty that such conduct is criminal.

WHEREFORE, the defendant prays that this matter be set down for a hearing after reasonable notice, and that the judgment be arrested on each and every ground hereof.
