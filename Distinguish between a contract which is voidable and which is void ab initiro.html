    Distinguish between a contract which is voidable and which is void ab initio?

    differences of a void contract and voidable contract

    See:

    VOID:

    A voidable contract doesn't have to be mutually voided by both parties. One party can void the contract.

    A voidable contract, unlike a void contract, is a valid contract. At most, one party to the contract is bound. The unbound party may repudiate the contract, at which time the contract is void.

    Example: A minor enters into a contract to purchase an iPod. As it is not something that is necessary for food or shelter, this contract can be voided at the MINOR's option but if the minor chooses to continue the contract, then it can be completed.

    VOID AB INITIO:

    The Latin phrase, void ab initio means "to be treated as invalid from the outset".

    Void ab initio. A contract is null from the beginning if it serious offends law or public policy in contrast to a contract which is merely voidable at the election of one of the parties to the contract. (from Black's Law Dictionary)

    An example of a void ab initio "contract" is a contract to commit some type of heinous crime..

      A voidable contract is one that can be voided by mutual agreement of the parties involved.

      A void ab initio contract is one that is not legally binding, in other words, an agreement that either party can walk away without any recourse. No mutual agreement is necessary.
