About The Speedy Trial Act

Did you know that the Government has just 70 days to bring you to trial?

Under the federal Speedy Trial Act, a defendant charged with a crime must be brought to trial within seventy (70) days following his initial appearance in court or within seventy (70) days from the date the Government files an indictment, whichever occurs last. If there is more than one defendant, all defendants must be brought to trial within seventy (70) days following the initial appearance of the last defendant to appear or within seventy (70) days from the date the Government files an indictment, whichever occurs last.

The so-called 70 day “trial clock” begins running the day after the last defendant appears in court or the day after the indictment is filed. Every day is counted including weekends and holidays. However, certain days may be excluded from computing these 70 days.

Under the Speedy Trial Act, there are two circumstances that exclude days from computation of the 70 days: (1) When either a defendant or the Government files any pre trial motion, the trial clock automatically stops and remains stopped until the trial court rules on the motion up to a maximum of thirty days in most instances; (2) When the trial court grants a continuance that is permitted under the Speedy Trial Act, the time allowed for the continuance is excluded from the 70 day computation.

While any pre trial motion automatically stops the 70 day trial clock, continuances do not. To stop the trial clock, a continuance must conform to the strict requirements set forth in the Speedy Trial Act. If the continuance does not conform, the time of the continuance is not excluded from the 70 day trial clock computation.

In the vast majority of cases in federal district courts across the country, continuances are granted that consistently fail to properly exclude time from the 70 day trial clock. Rarely does either side recognize the failure to exclude time and defendants in particular forfeit the opportunity to have their cases dismissed.

Because the docket for every case is controlled by the trial court, the Speedy Trial Act places the burden on the court to ensure that all defendants are brought to trial within 70 “non-excludable” days. It is not up to a defendant to bring himself to trial. However, it is incumbent on a defendant to monitor the 70 days trial clock in order to determine if and when his right to a speedy trial has been violated.

The Speedy Trial Act does not allow defendants to waive the right to a speedy trial before the expiration of the 70 non-excludable days when a trial is supposed to begin. That’s because the public also has a right to a speedy trial whenever anyone has been charged with a crime. And since a defendant can’t waive the public’s right to a speedy trial, a defendant can’t waive his right either. However, a defendant can waive his right to dismissal of all charges if he does not file a motion to dismiss the indictment before trial once he becomes aware that his right to speedy trial was violated.

The Sixth Amendment Right to Speedy Trial vs. The Speedy Trial Act

Because the guarantee of a speedy trial is one of the most basic rights preserved by our Constitution, it is a fundamental liberty embodied in the Bill of Rights. The protection afforded by this guarantee is activated only when a criminal prosecution has begun and extends only to those persons who have been 'accused' in the course of that prosecution.

So, what constitutes a speedy trial? Before 1974, it was relative. It varied from court to court. In extremely busy courts such as in Southern California or in Southern Florida, a 4 year wait might have been considered "speedy.” Whatever a court decided was or was not a speedy was the law.

Here is what the Sixth Amendment says: “In all criminal prosecutions, the accused shall enjoy the right to a speedy and public trial, by an impartial jury of the State and district wherein the crime shall have been committed, which district shall have been previously ascertained by law, and to be informed of the nature and cause of the accusation; to be confronted with the witnesses against him; to have compulsory process for obtaining witnesses in his favor, and to have the Assistance of Counsel for his defense.”

Finally, in 1974 Congress enacted the Speedy Trial Act to put teeth into the Sixth Amendment right to a speedy trial. The Speedy Trial Act set out specific time limits for when criminal trials must commence. It also provided for certain, limited, specific delays. But it also requires dismissal for a violation of the time limits. No longer would what constitutes speedy trial be determined by whim of a court. Rather, it would be determined according to the strict but plain provisions of the Speedy Trial Act.

Understanding and properly applying the Speedy Trial Act is probably the most potent defense weapon available to anyone charged with a federal crime and determining whether their was a speedy trial act violation.
